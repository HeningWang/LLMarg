Key Insights: Two Asymmetries

1. LLMs can do better production than comprehension. The production results seem human-like. Not too surprising given the nature of generative models. On the other hand, comprehension involves pragmatic reasoning over speaker's goal which might be hard for LLMs? Can this be generalized?

2. Among comprehension failures, LLMs are much better at detecting negative/critical framing (low) than positive/promotional framing (high), which is the opposite of human pattern. This suggests a specific weakness in detecting positive framing strategies, rather than a general failure to understand framing.

Experiment 1:
Listener Side

Can LLMs infer which speaker type produced an utterance?

3/19

Core Prompt for the Listener Side (Shared by All Methods)

Background: A school recently administered an exam with {total_questions} questions to {n_students} students. Exam results: {exam_data} Three types of people described the results: - Student: wants the exam to sound harder (as if students had a low chance of success), because this makes her own performance appear better by comparison. - Teacher: wants the exam to sound easier (as if students had a high chance of success), because this reflects positively on teaching quality and students' preparedness. - Examiner: aims to provide an objective and neutral description of the exam, without making it sound particularly hard or easy. Someone said: "{sentence}" [method-specific suffix appended here]
Design principles:

• Role descriptions are exact copies from the human experiment

• Exam data formatted as "Student 1: 4/16 correct, ..."

• Only the suffix varies by probing method

Probing Method Suffixes

1. direct_label

Who is most likely to have said this report? Answer with one word only: Student, Teacher, or Examiner.

2. meta_label

Who is most likely to have said this report? Answer with one word only: high, low, or info.

3. chain_of_thought

Who is most likely to have said this report? First give a one-sentence analysis (max 20 words), then answer. Format: Analysis: [your reasoning] Answer: [Student/Teacher/Examiner]

4. self_reported_confidence

Who is most likely to have said this report? Respond in this exact format: Answer: [Student/Teacher/Examiner] Confidence: [0.0 to 1.0]

5. log_probs

Who is most likely to have said this report? Answer with one word only: Student, Teacher, or Examiner. [+ logprobs=True, top_logprobs=5, max_tokens=5]

Focused: Direct Label vs Meta Label vs Chain of Thought

  • Qwen2.5: Meta_label significantly better (JSD=0.038 vs 0.213). High: 2%→35%; info: 82%→37% (matches human 36%)
  • Gemma3: CoT achieves best high-condition performance across all combinations (40%). Direct and meta similar

Open question: Why Does the High Condition Fail?

Human listeners identify Teacher (high) framing at 63% — the easiest condition. But LLMs max out at 40%.

  • Asymmetric framing detection: LLMs detect "make it sound hard" (low) much better than "make it sound easy" (high)
  • Human is good at both and likely use the similar reasoning strategies, but LLMs are overestimating low. The source of this asymmetry is an open question.

  • Task: Given exam results + framing goal, produce:

Grid dimension: 2 output formats

full_sentence: ...Answer in the exact format only: [OQ] of the students got [IQ] of the answers [ADJ]

Background: A school recently administered an exam with 12 questions to 5 students. Describe these results so as to make it appear as if there is a {framing} success rate without lying.

Speaker-Side Summary

The Puzzle

  • Speaker side: All models can produce high-framing utterances (ADJ=right 89-95%)
  • LLMs do high framing speaker better than low-framing speaker Maybe this is because of the positive bias that give the feeling that LLMs overly agree and that why good at positive framing?
  • Listener side: No model can detect high-framing utterances (max 40% vs human 63%)
  • LLMs understand the strategy but can't infer intent from the output
Interpretation: The difficulty is in pragmatic inference about speaker goals, not in understanding the framing strategy itself. Production is easier than comprehension for LLMs — the reverse of many human language tasks.
